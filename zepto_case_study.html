Context & Problem Statement

What's actually going on at Zepto?

I started this teardown after noticing that Zepto's App Store reviews were getting worse even though the app itself was getting more features. Most people in the quick commerce space were talking about the pricing wars and Zepto's EDLP move in November 2024. I wanted to go a layer deeper — to figure out why users were churning and if there was a real business cost attached to it.

In January 2026, Zepto quietly dropped its 10-minute delivery promise after worker protests and government pressure. That broke the news cycle, but I think it's actually a symptom of a bigger systemic failure — and it starts with customer support.

💡

The Problem Statement: Zepto's decision to cut customer support costs (moving from hybrid human+bot to bot-only) created a negative feedback loop where unresolved issues drive up churn, refund, and re-acquisition costs — far exceeding whatever was saved on support. This teardown explores that loop and proposes a fix.

4
Gaps & Root Cause Analysis

Three symptoms, one root cause

The more I dug in, the clearer it became that what looks like three separate UX issues is actually one systemic problem: Zepto's November 2024 decision to cut customer support costs to improve unit economics on paper.

The Vicious Cycle

📱

Inaccurate ETA Timer

App still shows "10 min" even though the promise was dropped Jan 2026. Delivery partner app crashes → orders don't complete → ETA gets stuck.

🤖

Bot-Only Support Can't Resolve Issues

User contacts Zap (Zepto's support bot). Bot provides preset options only. No escalation path to a human agent. Frustration compounds.

💸

Refund Delays + Zepto Cash Misfires

Payment is instant. Refunds take 5–6 working days. Zepto Cash shows discounts that disappear at checkout. Trust erodes fast.

📉

Churn → Re-acquisition Spend Goes Up

User switches to Blinkit/Instamart. Zepto spends ₹200–400 per user to win them back. Total cost of "saving" support > cost of having support.

🔁

To "Save More," Cuts Support Further

To hit short-term unit economics targets, Zepto cuts support more. Cycle repeats and gets worse. This is the death spiral.

What the broader market is saying

This isn't just a Zepto problem — but Zepto is the most visible example right now. Around the same time I was doing this research, Zomato was getting called out for the same thing:

"Zomato faces backlash for using AI over humans — journalist Abhishek Anand slammed Zomato after its AI support bot allegedly failed to connect him to a human during a claimed emergency."
LinkedIn post observed during research, 2026

The market is clearly telling us: AI support bots work for simple queries. They fail badly for anything emotional, urgent, or involving money. Zepto and Zomato are both learning this the hard way.

Business Impact & Unit Economics

The numbers tell a brutal story

This is the part I found most interesting. Everyone talks about support as a cost center. But when you actually trace where the money goes after cutting support, the math stops making sense pretty quickly.

Cost ComponentPer Order (₹)% of ₹550 AOVTrend

🧮

The trade-off in plain numbers: Zepto saved approximately ₹5–8/order on support costs. But refunds, churn re-acquisition, and CSAT coupons have risen by an estimated ₹16–26/order. That's a net negative of around ₹12–16 per order — and these are just the directly traceable costs. Brand damage from a 1.7 Trustpilot score is harder to put a number on but very real.

The deeper insight here: acquiring a new customer costs ₹200–400. Retaining one through good support costs roughly ₹12–18. Support isn't a cost to minimize — it's churn prevention at a fraction of re-acquisition spend. Zepto is essentially penny-wise and pound-foolish here.

Their ad revenue is growing (₹200M annual run rate in 2025, up from ₹40M the year before). Private labels are helping margins. But none of that helps if the trust problem keeps compounding. The EDLP + zero-fee model needs loyal, high-frequency customers to work — and right now Zepto is actively creating conditions for the opposite.

7
Proposed Solution — Support Redesign

The fix: Let the bot handle the easy stuff, let humans handle the rest

The solution isn't to get rid of Zap (Zepto's AI support bot). It's actually pretty decent for category-level routing. The problem is there's no escape hatch — once you're in the bot loop, you're stuck there even when your issue genuinely needs a human.

The proposed redesign adds one key thing: a "Connect to a Human" option that surfaces after 2–3 bot exchanges, or immediately for high-stakes issues like refunds, payment failures, or delayed orders. That's it. Small change, big impact on trust.

Smart Routing Logic

Bot handles: FAQs, order tracking, in-stock queries. Human handles: refunds, payment failures, complaints 2+ exchanges old.

⏱

SLA on Human Response

Target: connect to human within 3–5 minutes during peak, 10 minutes off-peak. ETA shown upfront so user knows what to expect.

💬

Context Handoff

When user escalates, the human agent gets full order context + conversation history. No repeating yourself. That alone reduces frustration significantly.

📊

Bot Keeps Learning

Every human-escalated conversation becomes a training case. Over 3–6 months, the bot handles a larger % of escalations automatically.

North Star Metric

30-day retention rate of users who had a support interaction. If someone contacted support and then still came back to order, that's the signal we fixed something real. Today that number is likely negative (support interactions likely increase churn). Target: flip it to neutral within 3 months, positive within 6.

Do-Not-Disturb Metric

Average order value and order frequency for non-escalated users. This change shouldn't affect the majority of customers who never need to contact support. If their behavior changes, something else broke.

9
Summary & Key Takeaways

The data suggests a consistent pattern: unresolved support friction may be generating downstream costs that outweigh the savings from automation alone

The data — from our survey, app reviews, and unit economics modelling — points in a consistent direction. Support friction appears to generate costs elsewhere through churn, refunds, and re-acquisition. The proposed intervention is focused and minimal: adding a human escalation path into the existing bot infrastructure. Small change, potentially significant impact on retention.
